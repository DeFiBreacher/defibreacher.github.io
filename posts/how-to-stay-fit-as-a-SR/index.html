Debug Your Body: A Fitness Guide for Tech People

You can write great codebase, audit smart contracts, and takedown systems at 2AM but when was the last time you debugged your own body?

Most tech people treat their health like a legacy codebase: ignore it until it breaks. By then, the damage is already done. And no, coffee cant fix that damage.

This is your guide to fixing that.

1. The Sitting Problem

The average tech person sits for 10–14 hours a day. Your body was not built for this.

Sitting for long periods compresses your spine, tightens your hip flexors, weakens your glutes, and cuts off circulation to your legs. This will going to cause you problem in the future. The fix is simple: move every hour.

Personally i have decided to go for long walks, i try to go over my faily step count which is at the time of writing this is 12k steps/day. You should also try to see how many steps you do, cause on a busy work day even i used to have around 2k steps/day. Set a timer for 50 minutes. When it goes off, stand up, walk around for 5–10 minutes, stretch your hips and back, then get back to work. This one habit alone will make a massive difference.

A standing desk is a great investment too — you don’t have to stand all day, just alternate between sitting and standing every hour or two.

2. Your Eyes Are Burning Out

My phone screentime will be less than 1.30 hour but i am afraid to look at the screentime of my pc, it would be so much, and thats the problem cause our jobs require us to stare/write/build codebases/apps etc. Also few of us like to play games to chill so screentime is throught the roof.

Follow the 20-20-20 rule:

Other things that help:

  • Set your screen brightness to match your room lighting.
  • Use night mode or a blue light filter after sunset.
  • Blink more consciously, screen users blink 3x less than normal.
  • Keep your monitor at arm’s length and slightly below eye level.

3. Posture — Your Spine Is Not a Suggestion

Bad posture is an epidemic in tech. Most people work hunched forward, chin jutting out, shoulders rounded, lower back unsupported.

The ideal desk setup:

  • Monitor at eye level, not tilted up or down.
  • Chair adjusted so your feet are flat on the floor.
  • Back fully supported by your chair.

4. Working Out When You Have No Time

The minimum effective dose: 4 days a week, 40–60 minutes, is enough to stay in great shape. The key is showing up consistently, not going all out and burning out.

5. Sleep — The Most Underrated Performance Tool

Most tech people are chronically sleep deprived and don’t even realize it. They’ve adapted to feeling tired and think it’s normal.

It’s not.

Ngl, feeling like sleeping is a luxury that i want to afford so badly.

Minimum: 7 hours. Optimal: 8–9 hours.

How to actually sleep better:

  • Stop screens 30–60 minutes before bed (blue light suppresses melatonin)
  • Keep your room cold, is optimal for sleep
  • Go to sleep and wake up at the same time every day, including weekends

6. Nutrition for a Sharp Mind

You can’t run demanding cognitive work on junk food. Your brain uses roughly 20% of your body’s energy — what you eat directly affects how well you think.

The basics:

Protein — Eat enough. Protein keeps you full, supports muscle, and stabilizes blood sugar. Aim for 0.8–1g per pound of bodyweight. Eggs, chicken, fish, Greek yogurt, legumes.

Don’t skip meals — Skipping meals crashes your blood sugar, which tanks focus and mood. Eat regularly.

Hydration — Even mild dehydration (1–2%) significantly impairs cognitive function. Drink water consistently throughout the day. If you’re relying on coffee for energy, you’re probably just dehydrated.

Limit ultra-processed food — These cause blood sugar spikes and crashes, leading to the afternoon energy slump most tech workers know too well.

Caffeine is a tool, not a crutch — One or two cups of coffee in the morning is fine. Chasing it throughout the day builds tolerance and disrupts sleep, leaving you more tired than before.

7. Mental Health & Burnout

Burnout is not a badge of honor. It is your system crashing.

Tech culture glorifies overwork — late nights, skipped weekends, “hustle”. But the human brain has limits. Push past them long enough and you stop being productive. You become slow, irritable, unmotivated, and prone to mistakes.

The Stack

If you do nothing else, do these five things:

  1. Move every hour — stand up, walk, stretch
  2. Exercise 3x a week — even 30 minutes of bodyweight work
  3. Sleep 7–8 hours — non-negotiable
  4. Drink water — before you reach for more coffee
  5. Go outside — sunlight and fresh air are free

Your body is the machine that runs everything else. Maintain it like you maintain your systems — proactively, not reactively.

Take care of yourself.
